About Me

My name is Joseph Farah. I am a college freshman, double majoring in physics and math at the University of Massachusetts Boston. I have an intense interest in all things science. I currently work at the Harvard Laboratory for Particle Physics and Cosmology, helping CERN build new muon detectors for instantaneous luminosity upgrades to the ATLAS detector at the Large Hadron Collider, planned for 2020.

During school, I enjoy challenging myself. Last year, I took 6 APs in a single year and scored 4's and 5's on all the tests, some of which were the hardest the CollegeBoard offers. Every year, I design and complete a science fair project. I won first place at my school fair 3 years in a row, 1st place in the Massachusetts State Science and Engineering Fair in 2016, and second place in the State Fair in 2017. At UMass Boston, I am currently enrolled in mostly sophomore level classes and conduct a significant amount self-studying on the side.

I similarly challenge myself outside of school. I am constantly on the lookout for difficult projects and internships to involve myself in. During high school, I interned at Tufts University and Harvard, where I dabbled in astrophysics, computational biology, robotics, statistics, and nuclear physics.

When I'm not ravenously trying to learn new things, you can find me helping in the community and the world. I volunteered for three years at the Museum of Science, teaching basic science concepts to children and adults alike. I was the Vice President of my school's chapter of the National Honors Society, coordinating charity efforts and helping raise money to support and raise awareness of the Yazidis, a minority religious and ethnic group of Northern Iraq that has been systematically decimated by ISIS and assistance for a local family, who lost their home and belongings in a fire.

I also developed a program called CORAL to document worldwide reef bleaching in real time and provide scientists and laymen alike with a tool to keep a weather eye on one of earth's most valuable and beautiful natural resources. The project was quite large and took almost half a year to complete, and culminated with a presentation of the software and its purpose to a crowd of over 150 people at Boston Python in July.

Work

Harvard Laboratory for Particle Physics and Cosmology

Research Intern
Supervisor: Prof. Melissa Franklin • June 2017 - Present

The team at Harvard's LPPC is currently working with CERN to develop muon detector prototypes for the ATLAS portion of the Large Hadron Collider. Specifically, we are working on the component known as the "New Small Wheel", which will replace the "Small Wheel", an important component that currently cannot accurately take data at the high energies the LHC will be achieving in the early 2020s. I work on the data-processing end of things, developing software to vastly increase the speed of analysis of detector data. I am also developing a low-voltage monitoring system, called Jupiter, to keep track of the detector prototype's (located right here in Cambridge) low voltage input, which we believe may be a significant source of noise and data corruption in our output. You can check out what I've done on our Github.

"A Lost World"

Above you can see my latest render, a photo-realistic render of an alien planet. The image is in 2k, so feel free to zoom in, inspect the many mountains, rivers, and cloud formations. Take a moment to zoom out and observe the gentle golden glow of the sunlight on the large lakes and oceans. Notice how the ground transitions into clouds and then into atmosphere, which gently fades into outer space.

The render took around a couple of weeks of on and off work to develop fully, and involves many texturing and compositing techniques. The method is original and somewhat similar to Andrew Price's Cycles Earth tutorial, all though you will notice the feel of the image is significantly different--I put an emphasis on beauty as opposed to strictly sticking to source material. All of the above elements, and more, were specifically targeted and developed in my render process to give maximum realism. I trawled through several BlenderArtists and NASA forums, hunting for tidbits of information that helped me learn how to add things from fresnel to atmospheric glow to specular.
